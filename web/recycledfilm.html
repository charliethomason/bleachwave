Summary: The history of Recycled Cinema and Recycled Film is somewhat complicated. Read more about Recycled Cinema here. In 2011, I decided to change the name of my film blog, recycledcinema.com, back to its original name, Recycled Film. The name “Recycled Cinema” seemed more specific to the movie industry and I was interested in writing about other topics, as well as helping displaying more of my artwork on-site. In November 2011, I re-launched the site as The Recycled Film and began to expanded my scope beyond film to include web development, Chicago happenings, punk and grunge music, and even my own artwork and photography.

The new site design was, at that time, the most advanced WordPress theme I had ever produced. It utilized many of the newest CSS3 technologies I had learned, as well as a custom-designed sidebar with dynamic category list, Instagram feed, and semantically-smart HTML structure for SEO value. The logo was based on an original drawing I scanned and cleaned up in Photoshop, and site’s black, white, and red color scheme was adopted from the Recycled Cinema theme. There was also originally a JQuery-based image slider for featured posts at the top of the home page, however it was removed due to low usage, as well as to increase browser compatibility.

The history of Recycled Film and Recycled Cinema includes many success stories, including live coverage of the 2009, 2010, and 2011 Sundance Film Festivals, exclusive access to press screenings and advanced previews, 2 articles that were also published in the Lexington, KY newspaper Ace Weekly (Gonzo and Milk), and a modest, yet very consistent readership of 100-500 hits per day. 80% of all posts from recycledcinema.com were edited and migrated to recycledfilm.net and proper 301 redirects were made for the remainder of posts not migrated. In fact, some of the most popular content on the site was much older content that continued to earn great search traffic, such as a character study of Charlie Chaplin’s Little Tramp and a comparison of Jim Jarmusch's Dead Man against the contemporary American western.

As of March 2012, my Google PageRank had increased to 4/10 and page traffic emphasis seemed to shift from my film writing, towards more of my newer stuff concerning web development and Code Academy, as well as my artwork and self-published photography book, Open Range. When I completed the Code Academy web development program at the end of March 2012, I decided that “Recycled Film” was no longer an accurate name for my personal website—given the now-liberally-expanded topic range—and began working on a new website, Bleachwave, that would enable me to expand my topic range even more, as well as include a complete portfolio of my work and eventually an online store.
